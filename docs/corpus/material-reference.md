# Material Reference

## Dimensional Lumber

### Nominal vs Actual Sizes

Lumber is sold by nominal (named) dimensions, but the actual milled size is smaller. Always model and cut using actual dimensions.

| Nominal | Actual |
|---------|--------|
| 1x2     | 3/4" x 1-1/2" |
| 1x3     | 3/4" x 2-1/2" |
| 1x4     | 3/4" x 3-1/2" |
| 1x6     | 3/4" x 5-1/2" |
| 1x8     | 3/4" x 7-1/4" |
| 1x10    | 3/4" x 9-1/4" |
| 1x12    | 3/4" x 11-1/4" |
| 2x2     | 1-1/2" x 1-1/2" |
| 2x3     | 1-1/2" x 2-1/2" |
| 2x4     | 1-1/2" x 3-1/2" |
| 2x6     | 1-1/2" x 5-1/2" |
| 2x8     | 1-1/2" x 7-1/4" |
| 2x10    | 1-1/2" x 9-1/4" |
| 2x12    | 1-1/2" x 11-1/4" |

The 1" nominal boards lose 1/4" in thickness. The 2" nominal boards lose 1/2". Widths 6" and under lose 1/2"; widths 8" and above lose 3/4".

### Board Types

**Whitewood / SPF (Spruce-Pine-Fir)**
The generic dimensional lumber sold at big-box stores. Often labeled "whitewood" or simply stocked unlabeled. Species varies by region and mill — you may get spruce, pine, or fir. Good for structural and utilitarian work. Knots and grain variation are common. Takes stain unevenly; paint or clear finish works better. Affordable.

**Select Pine**
Graded for appearance: fewer knots, straighter grain. Often sold as "select" or "#2 and better" pine. Good for painted furniture, shelving, and trim where a smooth finish matters. Costs more than whitewood but still affordable relative to hardwoods.

**Construction Grade (Framing Lumber)**
Graded for strength, not appearance. #1, #2, #3 grades. Expect warp, twist, and knots. Fine for structural use (framing, rough carpentry) where it won't be seen. Not suitable for finished furniture without extensive sorting and surfacing.

### Board Feet Formula

Board feet (BF) measures lumber volume. Used for pricing hardwoods and estimating material cost.

```
BF = (Thickness in inches × Width in inches × Length in inches) / 144
```

Or equivalently, with length in feet:

```
BF = (Thickness in inches × Width in inches × Length in feet) / 12
```

Examples:
- A 1x6 @ 8 feet: (1 × 6 × 8) / 12 = 4 BF
- A 2x4 @ 10 feet: (2 × 4 × 10) / 12 = 6.67 BF
- A 4/4 (1") x 8" x 6' hardwood board: (1 × 8 × 6) / 12 = 4 BF

Hardwoods are priced per board foot using nominal (rough) dimensions before surfacing, so you pay for material that gets milled away during S2S/S4S processing.

### Standard Lengths

Dimensional lumber is stocked in even-foot lengths: 6, 8, 10, 12, 14, 16 feet. Some stores carry 18 and 20 ft. Plan your cuts to minimize waste — lay out all parts on the available lengths before buying.

### Checking for Straightness

At the store, always sight down the board before buying:

- **Crown / bow**: End-to-end curve along the face. Lay the board flat and look down the length — one end will be high if crowned.
- **Twist / wind**: Two diagonal corners are high. Sight from one end; if opposite corners appear at different heights, the board is twisted.
- **Cup**: A curve across the width (face is concave or convex). Check by looking at the end grain cross-section.
- **Crook / edge bow**: The edge curves side to side. Sight down the edge like a rifle.

For furniture: reject boards with twist. Minor crown and cup can sometimes be flattened with a planer or hand plane, or managed by ripping to narrower widths. For structural use, mild bow and crook are acceptable if fastened flat.

### Acclimation

Wood is hygroscopic — it absorbs and releases moisture based on ambient humidity, and changes dimension as it does. Acclimation prevents movement and cracking after a project is built.

- **Rule of thumb**: Let lumber sit in the space where it will be used for at least 72 hours before cutting. For large projects or wood with high moisture content, 1-2 weeks is better.
- **Why it matters**: Lumber from a cold, damp warehouse brought into a heated shop can lose moisture rapidly and shrink or cup. Building with it before it stabilizes means joints open up and panels crack.
- **Check moisture content**: A cheap pin-type moisture meter is worthwhile. Target 6–8% MC for interior furniture in a heated home. Framing lumber from a big-box store often runs 15–19% MC.
- **Stack with stickers**: When storing lumber, place narrow spacers (stickers) between layers to allow airflow on all faces. This promotes even drying and reduces cupping.

## Plywood

### Thickness: Nominal vs Actual

Plywood is sold by nominal thickness, but actual thickness is almost always slightly less — and this matters when sizing dadoes and rabbets.

| Nominal | Actual (common) | Notes |
|---------|-----------------|-------|
| 1/4"    | 1/4" (0.250")   | Usually close to true 1/4" |
| 1/2"    | 1/2" (0.500") or 15/32" (0.469") | Varies by supplier |
| 3/4"    | 23/32" (0.719") | Standard big-box hardwood plywood |
| 3/4"    | 3/4" (0.750")   | Baltic birch, premium cabinet plywood |

**CRITICAL — Dado Sizing:**
Standard 3/4" plywood from big-box stores (Home Depot, Lowe's) measures approximately 23/32" (roughly 0.719"). If you cut a 3/4" dado to house this plywood, the panel will rattle loose. Always:
1. Measure your actual panel with calipers before cutting the dado.
2. Set your dado stack or router bit to match the measured thickness.
3. Test-fit in scrap before cutting your workpiece.

Baltic birch, MDF, and some premium cabinet plywood are closer to or exactly 3/4". When mixing sheet goods in a project, measure every panel — they may not be the same actual thickness even if sold as the same nominal size.

### Sheet Sizes

- **Standard sheet**: 4' x 8' (48" x 96")
- **Half sheet**: 4' x 4' or 2' x 8' (varies by store)
- **5x5 Baltic birch**: Baltic birch is typically sold in 5' x 5' (60" x 60") sheets, which affects cut layout significantly.
- **Oversized**: Some specialty suppliers stock 4x10 and 5x10 sheets.

### Grades

Plywood is graded per face: a grade is expressed as two letters (e.g., A-C), one for the front face and one for the back.

| Grade | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| A     | Smooth, paintable. Patches allowed but no open defects. Best appearance. |
| B     | Solid surface. Tight knots and minor splits allowed. |
| C     | Tight knots up to 1-1/2", knotholes to 1". Some splits. |
| D     | Knots and knotholes to 2-1/2". Used only as inner ply or unexposed back. |

Common grade combinations:
- **A-A**: Both faces furniture-quality. Use for visible panels on both sides (cabinet doors, tabletops).
- **A-C**: One good face. Standard cabinet box plywood — A face out, C face inside.
- **C-D**: Sheathing / construction grade. Not for finished work.

### Cabinet vs Construction Plywood

**Cabinet (hardwood) plywood**: Hardwood veneer faces (oak, maple, birch, etc.) over veneer core or MDF core. Sanded faces, consistent thickness, void-free core (in quality grades). Use for furniture, cabinet boxes, shelving.

**Construction plywood (sheathing)**: CDX or similar, softwood faces, gaps and voids in the core are common. Use for subfloors, roof sheathing, wall sheathing. Not suitable for finished furniture — voids blow out router bits and cause tearout.

**MDF core vs veneer core**: MDF core is flatter and machines more cleanly (good for painted work), but is heavier and doesn't hold screws at edges as well. Veneer core is lighter and holds fasteners better. For furniture with painted finish, MDF core is often preferred for flatness.

**Baltic birch**: All-birch veneer core with no voids, thin plies (typically 13-ply in 3/4"). Exceptionally flat and stable. Edges are attractive and can be left exposed. The standard for shop fixtures, drawer boxes, and high-quality painted furniture. More expensive; sold in 5x5 sheets.

### Edge Treatment Options

Plywood edges show the core and are not attractive raw. Common treatments:

- **Edge banding (iron-on veneer tape)**: Thin veneer strip with hot-melt adhesive. Applied with a household iron. Quickest method. Trim flush with a trimmer or chisel. Works well for shelf edges; less durable on high-traffic edges.
- **Solid wood edge banding (glued strip)**: Rip a strip of solid wood, glue and clamp to the edge. More durable, can be routed with a profile, and looks better on finished pieces. Width is typically 1/4"–3/4" to cover the ply edge and allow flush trimming.
- **Router or chamfer**: A small chamfer or roundover on the edge softens it and removes the sharp veneer corner, even without covering the edge. Not a "treatment" per se but reduces visible layering.
- **Edge filler / skim coat**: Apply wood filler or a skim coat of joint compound (on MDF), sand smooth, prime, paint. Works for painted projects where edge grain is hidden under finish.
- **Leave exposed (Baltic birch)**: Baltic birch edges are clean and attractive. Sand, apply a couple coats of finish, and leave visible intentionally — common in modern/Scandinavian furniture.

## Wood Species

### Indoor Species

| Species   | Janka Hardness (lbf) | Workability | Relative Cost | Stain Acceptance |
|-----------|----------------------|-------------|---------------|------------------|
| Pine (Eastern White) | 380 | Easy — soft, cuts and sands fast. Dents easily. | Low | Poor — blotchy. Pre-conditioner required. |
| Pine (Southern Yellow) | 1,225 | Moderate — harder than white pine, more resin. | Low–Medium | Poor — very blotchy, resinous. |
| Poplar | 540 | Excellent — stable, machines cleanly, minimal tearout. | Low–Medium | Poor — green/gray streaks absorb unevenly. Best painted. |
| Red Oak | 1,290 | Good — open grain, machines well, some tearout. | Medium | Excellent — absorbs stain evenly, very responsive. |
| Hard Maple | 1,450 | Moderate — hard, burns easily on router/saw. Sharp tools required. | Medium–High | Difficult — very dense, resists penetration. Gel stains or dye work better. |
| Black Walnut | 1,010 | Excellent — works beautifully with hand and power tools. | High | Excellent — rich natural color, minimal staining needed. |
| Cherry (American) | 950 | Excellent — fine grain, finishes beautifully. | High | Good — can be blotchy when young; pre-conditioner helps. Darkens dramatically with light exposure. |

**Notes on indoor species:**

- **Pine**: Ideal for painted furniture and beginner projects. Inexpensive and forgiving. For stain projects, always use a pre-conditioner (Minwax Wood Conditioner or similar) and expect to test your stain color on scrap.
- **Poplar**: The professional's choice for painted furniture. Stable, affordable, machines cleanly. The natural green/gray streaks make it unattractive for stained work, but under paint it's excellent.
- **Red Oak**: The most forgiving hardwood for beginners. Widely available, predictable, and takes stain beautifully. Open grain requires a grain filler for a glass-smooth finish (like on tabletops), but for most furniture it looks great with minimal prep.
- **Hard Maple**: Dense and durable — great for workbenches, butcher block tops, and cutting boards. The density makes it resistant to stain; use dye or pigment wiping stains rather than oil-based penetrating stains.
- **Walnut**: Expensive but works easily and looks stunning with minimal finish. A clear oil or wiping varnish is all it needs. Worth the cost for show pieces.
- **Cherry**: Prices itself between oak and walnut. The stand-out property is photosensitive aging — it starts light pink/tan and deepens to a rich reddish-brown over months of light exposure. Build cherry pieces knowing they will look completely different in a year.

### Outdoor Species

| Species | Rot Resistance | Fastener Holding | Notes |
|---------|---------------|------------------|-------|
| Southern Yellow Pine (SYP) | Low (untreated) | Excellent | Dense and strong. Common in the South. Takes pressure treatment well. Use PT version for ground contact. |
| Douglas Fir | Low–Moderate | Good | West Coast standard for framing and outdoor structures above grade. Straight-grained, strong-to-weight ratio. |
| Pressure-Treated (PT) Pine | High (Class 1 for ground contact) | Good | Treated with preservatives (now ACQ or copper azole — not the old arsenic-based CCA). Use for ground contact, deck framing, posts. Wet when new — let dry before painting. Use stainless or hot-dipped galvanized fasteners to avoid corrosion. |
| Western Red Cedar | High | Moderate — soft, screws can strip | Naturally rot-resistant oils. Lightweight. Classic for decking, siding, outdoor furniture. Attractive grain. Will silver to gray if left unfinished; apply oil or sealant to maintain color. |
| Redwood | High | Moderate | Similar to cedar in rot resistance and appearance. Regional availability (West Coast). More expensive than cedar in most markets. |

**Fastener note for outdoor species**: When using pressure-treated lumber, standard galvanized fasteners corrode quickly due to the copper in modern PT preservatives. Use hot-dipped galvanized (not electroplated), stainless steel, or fasteners rated specifically for ACQ/copper-azole treated lumber.

### Red-Toned vs Yellow-Toned 2x Lumber

This distinction matters when your 2x lumber will be stained or when mixing boards in a visible project.

**Yellow-toned (SPF, white fir, hem-fir)**: Lighter, more neutral color. Common in the Mountain West and Pacific Northwest. Stains more evenly and produces a cleaner result with light stains.

**Red-toned (SYP, "pink" southern pine)**: Distinctly pink to reddish-brown heartwood. Common in the South and Southeast. The resin content and color variation make staining more difficult and blotchy. Under a dark stain it may look fine; under a light or natural stain the red tones compete.

**Practical implication**: If you're building a project from 2x construction lumber and plan to stain it, sort the stack at the store and select boards with consistent color tones. Mixing red-toned and yellow-toned boards in a stained project produces a noticeably mismatched result.

### Hardwood vs Softwood: When to Use Which

**Use softwood (pine, poplar, SPF, etc.) when:**
- The project will be painted — softwoods paint beautifully and cost a fraction of hardwood.
- It's a utility or shop project (workbench, storage, jigs).
- It's a beginner project focused on learning joinery and technique rather than material.
- You're building large structural pieces where weight and cost matter (bed frames, built-ins, bookshelves).
- Budget is the primary constraint.

**Use hardwood (oak, maple, walnut, cherry, etc.) when:**
- The project will be stained or have a natural clear finish where wood figure and grain are visible.
- The piece will see heavy use or abuse (tabletops, chair seats, cutting boards, tool handles).
- Durability is required — hardwoods resist denting, scratching, and wear that softer woods won't survive.
- You want the project to last generations or carry heirloom quality.
- The joinery is visible (exposed dovetails, through-tenons) and you want it to read clearly.

**Mixed approach**: Many furniture projects combine both. A common pattern is hardwood for face frames, door fronts, and exposed edges, with cabinet-grade plywood for panels and softwood or secondary hardwood (poplar) for interior parts and drawer sides. This controls cost while putting quality material where it shows.
