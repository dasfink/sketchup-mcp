# Mortise and Tenon Workbench -- Woodgears.ca

**Source:** https://woodgears.ca/workbench/quick.html
**Archetype:** frame-panel
**Joints used:** Mortise and tenon (3/4" mortises, ~1.5" tenons), dados, screws
**Material:** Scrap 2x4s and 2x6s (softwood, salvaged), salvaged door (top), used drawer units

## Joint Decisions

This project uses **mortise and tenon joints** as the primary structural connection -- a step up from the author's other workbench that used dowels. The 3/4" mortises were cut using a pantorouter with a straight bit, and the tenons were nearly 1.5" long (requiring two passes due to 1.25" bit depth limit).

The key design insight: the front rail uses "two through mortises cut into it...to fit over the tenons in the frames." This through-tenon approach allows the rail to slide onto the frame assemblies, creating a semi-knockdown structure. The author specifically "resisted gluing" the whole workbench because "the whole workbench was rigid enough without" -- demonstrating that well-fitted M&T joints can be self-supporting through friction alone.

**Dado joints** were used for the back rail, providing a recessed seat that resists lateral movement. Combined with pre-drilled, countersunk screws, this creates a permanent but clean connection.

**Screws only** attached the tabletop (salvaged door), keeping it replaceable without destroying the base structure.

Trade-offs: M&T provides more rigidity than the dowels used in the author's simpler workbench, but requires a pantorouter or mortising machine. The unglued through-tenon front rail is a clever compromise -- strong enough for a workbench but disassemblable if needed.

## Construction Notes

1. Crosscut 2x6 lengths for legs using a table saw sled
2. Rip pieces to create legs
3. Plane 1mm off all sides using a jointer
4. Cut 3/4" mortises and tenons with pantorouter
5. Assemble side frames with mortise-and-tenon joints
6. Cut dados for back rail (stacking two saw blades for wider cuts)
7. Attach back rail with pre-drilled, countersunk screws
8. Install front rail with through mortises (slides over frame tenons)
9. Add lower support members
10. Assemble drawer units on site
11. Attach tabletop with screws only

**Problems:** The author "goofed a bit with where I put the lower rungs," requiring fabricated support pieces to properly position a side-to-side member for drawer unit support. Drawer depth proved suboptimal -- "only 60% as deep as they could be."

**Tips:** "A sturdy sled makes cross cutting an eight-foot 2x6 easy." Plane one side of questionable scrap lumber first to avoid embedded debris (nails, grit). Use slower RPM settings for cleaner chips and reduced noise/dust. Apply generous glue coverage to both mortise and tenon surfaces. Pre-drill shank holes in fastened members to prevent splitting. Lay out screw holes systematically for better appearance.

## Relevance to Skill

This project pairs well with the author's dowel-based workbench as a **direct comparison of M&T vs. dowel approaches** for the same furniture type. The M&T version is more rigid and supports heavier use, but requires more tooling (pantorouter). This comparison should inform our joint selection: when the user has a pantorouter or mortising machine, recommend M&T; when they have only a drill press, recommend the dowel approach. The unglued through-tenon technique is a valuable pattern for semi-knockdown workshop furniture -- strong enough through friction alone. The scrap-lumber philosophy also validates that joint selection should not assume premium materials.
