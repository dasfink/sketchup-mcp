# Kitchen Chairs -- Woodgears.ca

**Source:** https://woodgears.ca/kitchen_chairs/
**Archetype:** chair
**Joints used:** Mortise and tenon
**Material:** Hardwood (species not specified), varying thicknesses

## Joint Decisions

The author chose **mortise and tenon joints** throughout -- the traditional and strongest choice for chair construction. Mortises were cut into legs (on both flat surfaces and edges) to receive tenons from apron rails and back components. This is the gold standard for chairs because chairs endure racking forces from all directions, and mortise-and-tenon joints resist these forces better than any other wood joint.

A critical sequencing decision: mortises were cut "after turning because they would have added to imbalance of the workpiece" on the lathe. This shows how joint cutting must be integrated with the overall machining sequence.

Trade-off insight: One chair failed from moisture exposure -- "being kept in a wet basement, and the seat expanded too much and pushed the joints apart." This reveals that even the strongest wood joint has environmental limits, which is relevant for outdoor or basement furniture recommendations.

## Construction Notes

1. Create CAD model and 1:1 templates using BigPrint program on card stock
2. Lay out parts on boards, considering grain orientation and wood defects
3. Cut parts from lumber
4. Flatten and smooth pieces using jointer, belt sander, and thickness planer
5. Mill rectangular apron rail components
6. Turn front legs; glue two pieces together for adequate thickness
7. Cut mortises into legs (after turning)
8. Process remaining components and assemble

**Tips:** Cut parts before flattening to minimize thickness loss. Use card stock templates -- "stiff enough that I can trace around them with a pencil without distorting them." Reference finished spindles visually rather than measuring repeatedly.

**Problems:** Forgotten lubrication caused wooden lathe bearings to smoke. Layout didn't account for bandsaw cutting constraints. Moisture-induced failure in one chair.

## Relevance to Skill

This is the **definitive chair archetype** example. Chairs are the hardest furniture to joint correctly because they endure dynamic racking loads. The universal use of mortise-and-tenon here validates our taxonomy's recommendation of M&T as the primary chair joint. The moisture failure case is valuable for our environmental factor logic -- we should flag that chairs in damp environments need either: (a) finish protection, (b) mechanical fastener backup, or (c) alternative materials. The template-based workflow also informs our SketchUp-to-shop-floor pipeline.
