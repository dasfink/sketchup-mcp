# Student Desk -- Woodgears.ca

**Source:** https://woodgears.ca/student-desk/
**Archetype:** flat-pack / modular
**Joints used:** Mortise and tenon, lap joints, rabbet joints, dowel connections with steel pins
**Material:** Maple (frame), 3/8" OSB/Aspenite (desktop)

## Joint Decisions

This project is a masterclass in choosing joints for **knockdown/portable furniture**:

- **Mortise and tenon joints** connect the side frames, but the author notes these "are not really strength critical" -- dowels would work equally well. The M&T choice here is about precision alignment rather than strength.
- **Lap joints** where crossbraces overlap in the middle, though intentionally "loose" with no actual attachment -- gravity and geometry hold everything.
- **Rabbet joints** let the OSB desktop sit within the maple frame, increasing gluing surface area.
- **Dowel connections with steel pins** (modified nails with ground-off points) secure crossbraces to the frame via tapered dowels. The pins prevent the dowels from lifting out during transport.

The entire desk requires "no tools" to disassemble and is "held together by gravity." This is a remarkable demonstration of designing joints for portability while maintaining sturdiness.

## Construction Notes

1. Built rectangular side frames connected with mortise-tenon joints
2. Added rungs sitting in notches on frames to support the desktop
3. Attached crossbraces via dowels and pins for lateral stability
4. Created the OSB desktop with maple border and rabbet joinery
5. Reinforced corner frame with diagonal 45-degree dowels
6. Applied seven coats of varnish on the OSB top

**Tips:** The 1cm ridge created by the thicker frame locking into rungs ensures alignment and prevents sliding. For varnishing, one extremely thick final coat produces better results than multiple thin coats on horizontal surfaces.

## Relevance to Skill

This is the strongest **flat-pack/knockdown archetype** example in the corpus. The joint selection is driven entirely by the portability requirement -- every joint is chosen for disassembly rather than maximum strength. This maps directly to our joint taxonomy's "knockdown" category and validates the need for a "portability" factor in joint selection. The gravity-held lap joints and pin-retained dowels are patterns we should encode: when the user specifies "needs to be portable" or "will be moved frequently," recommend these approaches over permanent glue joints.
