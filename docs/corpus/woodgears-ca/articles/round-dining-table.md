# Round Dining Table -- Woodgears.ca

**Source:** https://woodgears.ca/table/round_dining.html
**Archetype:** frame-panel
**Joints used:** Mortise and tenon, dowel joints (5/8"), 45-degree miter joints
**Material:** Laminated spruce (2x6 lumber), originally pine (6cm thick)

## Joint Decisions

This project is notable for showing how the author's joint preferences evolved over time:

**Original design (1996):** Used **mortise and tenon joints** for the curved leg-to-crosspiece connections, but discovered that "the tenons have to intersect" inside the leg where multiple rails meet at 45-degree angles. The workaround was cutting notches to create "a sort of box joint inside the table leg" -- an ad hoc solution that worked but was unnecessarily complex.

**Improved design:** The author recommends **5/8" dowel joints** as superior for this application, with "holes...offset by 2cm from each other along the leg, so that the dowels can intersect." This elegantly solves the intersecting-tenon problem by staggering the connections vertically.

**45-degree miter joints** where curved legs meet create clean visual lines. The author emphasizes checking that "two 45-degree angles together form a 90-degree angle" before final cuts -- an error in the original build required "a lot of force on final assembly" to close gaps.

For **cross pieces to tabletop**, a "relatively shallow mortise and tenon joint" was used, though the author notes "two 5/8" dowels should do the trick too." The continuous part of the cross member should run "perpendicular to the grain of the table top, so that it can help to hold the table top flat."

## Construction Notes

1. Laminate two 2x6 boards with flat butt joint for leg blanks
2. Rough-cut leg shape, leaving ~0.5cm margin
3. Plane on jointer to flatten
4. Glue boards to both sides of leg piece (building up thickness)
5. Trim excess; re-outline shape
6. Plane back surface flat as reference
7. Final outline cut with bandsaw
8. Plane to 60mm thickness
9. Cut 45-degree miters (2cm x 2cm triangles removed from leg ends)
10. Drill dowel holes at 45-degree angles using horizontal boring jig
11. Sand edges, apply 1/2" radius router bit
12. Create cross pieces with offset holes
13. Glue assembly, checking for square
14. Assemble frame in two stages: C-shaped first, then I-shaped, for adjustment flexibility

**Dimensions:** Legs 65cm high, 8.5cm wide along curve, 60mm thick. Cross pieces 185mm long, 120mm wide, 30mm thick. Tabletop 35mm thick. Final height ~72cm (standard dining table range 71-75cm). Seats seven comfortably; eight is cramped.

**Problems:** Laminated joint created unevenness requiring jointer flattening. Pencil marks erased during planing, requiring re-tracing. Drill press tilting table created obstruction from vertical column, requiring a horizontal boring jig. Original M&T design had intersecting tenons. Miter angle inaccuracy in original build required excessive force at assembly.

**Tips:** Laminating legs from multiple pieces allows "orienting the grain a little better, for more strength." Leave sanding until late to avoid embedding nicks. "Do the routing after the sanding, so that the guide roller won't end up following any unevenness." Assemble in two stages (C and I configurations) to allow clamp adjustment if dimensions don't align perfectly.

## Relevance to Skill

This is an excellent case study in **joint evolution through experience**. The author's shift from M&T to dowels for the leg connections -- driven by the intersecting-tenon problem -- validates our taxonomy's position that dowels can be superior to M&T in specific geometric situations. The staggered-offset dowel technique for compound angles should be encoded as a recommendation when our system detects joints meeting at non-right angles. The two-stage assembly strategy (C-shape then I-shape) is a valuable assembly planning pattern for symmetrical frames. The grain-orientation advice for cross pieces (perpendicular to tabletop grain for flatness) is exactly the kind of practical knowledge our skill should surface during design review.
