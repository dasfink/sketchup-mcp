# Bed Frame -- Woodgears.ca

**Source:** https://woodgears.ca/bed/build.html
**Archetype:** frame-panel
**Joints used:** Dowel joints (5/8"), screw attachment, glued & screwed rails
**Material:** Spruce construction lumber (2x8, 2x6, 2x10, 2x2, 2x3), recycled box-spring slats

## Joint Decisions

The author chose **5/8" dowel joints** for connecting horizontal members to bedposts, drilling through both pieces simultaneously using a jig. This approach avoids the precision challenges of aligning blind holes -- by drilling through both pieces at once, alignment is guaranteed even with freehand drilling. Dowels provide good shear strength for the horizontal-to-vertical connections in the headboard and footboard.

**Screw attachment** (six 2.5" #8 screws per side) was used for side rails to posts, making the bed disassemblable for moving. The author noted in retrospect that #10 screws would have been slightly better for the load.

A **2x2 support beam** was screwed and glued to the bottom edge of the 2x8 side rails, with blocks every 6" to prevent slat movement -- a simple but effective approach for distributing mattress load.

Trade-offs: The dowel approach is simpler than mortise-and-tenon but still provides good rigidity. Screws for side rails sacrifice some rigidity for practical disassembly. The total material cost was under $30-50.

## Construction Notes

1. Selected and planed construction lumber using a power planer
2. Added quarter-inch chamfers to all exposed edges using the planer's V-notch infeed
3. Created headboard and footboard by joining 2x3 posts to horizontal members with dowels
4. Glued second 2x3 to posts to complete post assembly
5. Attached 2x2 support beam to side rails
6. Screwed side rails into notched areas of posts
7. Installed recycled lumber slats from a dismantled box spring

**Problems:** A cupped board was flattened by clamping during dowel drilling. Planing ridges from overlapping passes on wider boards required belt sanding. Small gaps appeared between headboard horizontals and posts, solved with improvised clamping extensions. A second 2x3 misaligned by ~1mm during glue-up, corrected by planing.

**Tips:** Construction lumber can be forced straight during assembly despite light twists. An alternative approach uses 4" long wood screws instead of dowels for even simpler assembly.

## Relevance to Skill

This project demonstrates the **frame archetype** with practical joint trade-offs. The dowel-through-both-pieces technique maps directly to our dowel joint taxonomy -- it's a beginner-friendly method that produces reliable results. The mixed joinery approach (dowels for permanent assemblies, screws for knockdown connections) is a pattern we should encode in our joint selection logic: when a project needs to be moved/disassembled, use mechanical fasteners for those specific connections while using stronger joinery elsewhere.
