# Rectangular Kitchen Table -- Woodgears.ca

**Source:** https://woodgears.ca/table/build_table.html
**Archetype:** frame-panel
**Joints used:** Mortise and tenon (loose tenons), dowel pegs, screws with clearance slots
**Material:** Black walnut (legs, salvaged from library furniture), birch (apron rails), mahogany and birch (wood-tiled top)

## Joint Decisions

The author went through a deliberate elimination process for the leg-to-apron connection, rejecting several approaches before arriving at **loose mortise and tenon joints** with an intermediary short wooden piece:

- **Traditional mortise and tenon** (legs directly to aprons) was rejected because "can't be taken apart for moving."
- **Diagonal brace with single bolt** was rejected because it creates instability and "isn't a good way for the vertical load to be transferred."
- **45-degree angled screw holes** were rejected because the drill "pulls to the side too much when it enters the wood."

The chosen solution: mortise and tenon joints connect legs to short wooden blocks, and then screws through those blocks attach to the long apron rails. This hybrid approach provides the rigidity of M&T where it matters (leg attachment) with the disassembly capability of screws where it matters (rail connection).

**Dowel pegs** were added at rail ends to "transfer vertical load onto the legs" -- a structural reinforcement that prevents the screws from bearing all the weight.

For the tabletop attachment, the author created **screw holes with clearance slots** that are "expanded near the top, to allow latitude...caused by shrinkage and expansion." This is critical for solid wood (not plywood) due to "several millimeters" of cross-grain movement with humidity changes.

The author initially attempted doweling rails directly into legs using a horizontal boring jig, but "the holes...ended up being at just a little bit of an angle." He adapted by cutting mortises through the angled holes using a mortising machine -- a pragmatic recovery from a mistake.

## Construction Notes

1. Taper legs using a jointer with spacer technique
2. Cut mortises in legs and short wooden connector pieces
3. Create loose tenons for leg-to-block connections
4. Glue mortise/tenon assemblies (creating two-leg units)
5. Attach long apron rails with screws through short blocks
6. Insert dowel pegs in rail ends for vertical load transfer
7. Glue ledge strips inside end rails
8. Drill screw holes with clearance slots for tabletop attachment
9. Assemble wood-tiled tabletop

**Problems:** The doweling attempt failed due to slightly angled holes, even with a horizontal boring jig. The author recovered by converting the angled dowel holes into mortises. This is an excellent example of adaptive problem-solving in the shop.

**Tips:** Use mortising machines for rigidity over hand drilling. Add dowel pegs to transfer vertical loads rather than relying solely on screws. Always create expandable screw holes for solid wood tabletops to accommodate seasonal movement.

## Relevance to Skill

This project demonstrates the **frame archetype** with a sophisticated analysis of joint trade-offs. The author's elimination process for leg joints is exactly the kind of reasoning our joint selection logic should replicate: consider disassembly needs, load transfer, drilling precision requirements, and available tools. The loose-tenon approach (mortises in both pieces, separate tenon) is a modern technique that deserves its own entry in our joint taxonomy -- it combines M&T strength with better tolerance for imprecision. The seasonal wood movement accommodation via clearance slots is a critical detail our system should flag whenever solid wood tabletops are specified.
