# Finish Guide for Furniture Projects

Finish selection affects joint choice, material choice, and build tolerances. Choose the finish before cutting wood.

---

## Paint Workflow

Based on Ana White's finishing guide. Paint is the most forgiving finish and the best choice for Tier 0 builds.

### Steps

1. **Fill holes** with wood filler. Overfill slightly — filler shrinks as it dries. Fill pocket holes, screw holes, nail holes, and any gaps at joints.
2. **Sand with medium grit** (120-150). Focus on joints — they must be flush and smooth. Sand filler flush with the surface. Do not round over edges yet unless intended.
3. **Clean** with vacuum first, then a damp (not wet) cloth. Dust in paint is the most common amateur mistake.
4. **Prime.** Always prime, even with "self-priming" paint. Primer seals the wood, prevents bleed-through (especially with pine knots), and gives paint a uniform surface to grip. Use a stain-blocking primer (Zinsser BIN or Kilz Original) on knotty pine.
5. **Paint with foam roller** for flat surfaces. Use a foam applicator or small brush for inside corners and edges. Foam rollers leave minimal texture compared to nap rollers.
6. **Build color** with 3-4 thin coats. Sand lightly (220 grit) between coats to knock down any nibs or dust bumps. Thin coats dry faster and resist runs.

### Common mistakes
- Skipping primer (causes bleed-through and uneven sheen)
- Thick coats (causes runs, takes forever to cure, stays soft)
- Sanding too aggressively between coats (cuts through to primer)

## Stain Workflow

Stain is less forgiving than paint. Gaps, filler, and sloppy joints show.

### Steps

1. **Test on scrap first.** This is not optional. Stain color varies wildly by species, grain density, and number of coats. Pine stains blotchy without conditioner.
2. **Sand to 150-180 grit.** Going finer (220+) can close pores and reduce stain absorption, producing lighter color than expected.
3. **Apply pre-stain conditioner** on softwoods (pine, SPF, poplar). This evens absorption and reduces blotching. Follow manufacturer's timing — too long and it stops working.
4. **Apply stain** with a foam applicator or lint-free rag. Work with the grain. Cover one surface or component at a time.
5. **Wipe excess** before it dries tacky. The longer stain sits, the darker it gets. Consistency comes from consistent wipe timing.
6. **Apply polyurethane topcoat** for any high-use surface (table tops, shelves, anything that gets touched). Water-based poly dries faster and stays clear. Oil-based poly adds warmth but yellows over time.

### Common mistakes
- Not testing on scrap (the can lid color is a lie)
- Expecting wood filler to take stain (it does not — it will show as a different color)
- Skipping conditioner on pine (causes ugly blotching)
- Forgetting topcoat (stain alone has zero durability)

## Natural / Oil Workflow

Oil finishes show every flaw and every beauty mark. Joints must be clean. This is the finish for Tier 2-3 builds with quality hardwood.

### Steps

1. **Sand progressively to 220 grit.** Start at 80 or 120 (depending on surface condition), move through 150, then 220. Each grit removes scratches from the previous grit.
2. **Raise the grain** (optional but recommended). Wipe surface with a damp cloth, let dry, then sand lightly with 220. This prevents the grain from raising when oil is applied.
3. **Apply oil** (Danish oil, tung oil, or boiled linseed oil) with a lint-free rag or foam applicator. Flood the surface generously.
4. **Wait 15-30 minutes**, then wipe off all excess. Oil that pools in corners or joints will become sticky and never cure properly.
5. **Repeat for 2-3 coats**, waiting the manufacturer's recommended time between coats (usually 24 hours).
6. **Buff the final coat** lightly with 0000 steel wool or a brown paper bag for a silky feel.

### Common mistakes
- Not wiping off excess (causes sticky spots that never cure)
- Using too few coats (wood looks dry and unprotected)
- Using oil on softwood expecting a "hardwood look" (oil amplifies grain character — pine still looks like pine)

## Outdoor Finish

Outdoor furniture faces UV, rain, temperature swings, and humidity cycling. No finish lasts forever outdoors.

### Steps

1. **Use exterior-rated products only.** Interior poly, stain, or oil will fail within one season.
2. **Apply exterior stain/sealer** following manufacturer directions. Penetrating stains (not film-forming) handle wood movement better.
3. **Reapply every 1-2 years.** Budget for maintenance. Furniture that looks great in year one and terrible in year three needed recoating in year two.
4. **Consider leaving wood unfinished** if using naturally durable species (cedar, white oak, teak). They gray with a silver patina that some find attractive.

### Common mistakes
- Using interior products outdoors (peels within months)
- Film-forming finishes on outdoor wood (trap moisture, cause rot)
- Assuming "one and done" — outdoor finishes are maintenance, not permanent

## How Finish Affects Joint Selection

The chosen finish constrains which joints make sense. Recommending dovetails on a painted box is wasteful. Recommending pocket screws on an oil-finished walnut cabinet is ugly.

| Finish | Joint Implications |
|---|---|
| Paint | Pocket holes can be filled and hidden. Butt joints disappear under filler and paint. Precision still matters (gaps show through paint), but cosmetic joints are unnecessary. Best match for Tier 0 joints. |
| Stain | Pocket holes need wood plugs (face-grain plugs from the same board). Wood filler will not take stain evenly — it always shows. Joints must be tighter than paint-grade. Dado and rabbet joints work well (hidden by assembly). |
| Natural/oil | Cleanest joints required. Dovetails and M&T become visual features, not hidden structure. Gaps are unforgivable. This finish rewards Tier 2-3 joinery skills and tools. |
| Outdoor | Mechanical fasteners (bolts, screws, structural screws) preferred. Glue joints may fail from moisture cycling. Stainless steel or coated hardware only. |

### Decision rule

If the user says "I'm going to paint it," do not recommend dovetails or exposed M&T. Recommend the simplest joint that meets structural requirements. The finish dictates the appropriate joint complexity.

## How Finish Affects Material Selection

Do not recommend walnut for a painted project. Do not recommend construction-grade SPF for an oil-finished project.

| Finish | Material Guidance |
|---|---|
| Paint | Pine and SPF are ideal. Poplar is excellent (cheap, stable, paints beautifully). Hardwood is overkill — you are paying for grain character you will cover up. Plywood (cabinet-grade birch) is perfect for painted casework. |
| Stain | Need consistent, even grain. Poplar (budget stain-grade, takes dark stains well). Red oak (pronounced grain, stains predictably). Walnut (beautiful but expensive — skip stain and use oil instead). Avoid pine unless using pre-stain conditioner. |
| Natural/oil | The wood IS the finish. Cherry (ages to deep red), walnut (rich brown), white oak (golden with ray fleck), maple (light and clean). Choose species for their color and grain. These woods justify the cost because the finish shows them off. |
| Outdoor | Cedar, redwood, white oak, or pressure-treated lumber. Match species to budget: cedar for visible surfaces, PT lumber for structure. Teak for high-budget outdoor projects. |

### Decision rule

Ask the user about finish early in the design process. Material and joint recommendations both depend on the answer. A painted pine bookshelf with pocket screws and an oiled walnut bookshelf with through-dados are both correct designs for their context — but mixing the pairs (oiled pine with pocket screws, painted walnut with dados) wastes either effort or money.
