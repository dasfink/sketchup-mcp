# Wood Choosing Guide

Source: Ana White

## Indoor -- Common Boards (Whitewood)

Pine, spruce, or other light-toned coniferous species. These are the go-to for beginner indoor projects.

**Pros:** Inexpensive, eco-friendly, available in standard dimensional sizes, soft and easy to cut, lighter tone that accepts stain and bonds well with paint.

**Picking good boards:** Sight down the length like an arrow on a bow to check straightness. Inspect for cracks, checks, and large loose knots. Minor imperfections can be cut around.

**Acclimating:** Common boards absorb moisture and will swell or shrink. Always acclimate lumber to your indoor environment before building. Check that dimensions match your plans right before you start.

## Hardwoods

More expensive, denser, and harder to work. May need special tools. Reserve for experienced woodworkers.

## Plywood

### 1/4" Plywood

Used for backs of bookcases, dressers, and cabinets, and for drawer bottoms. Adds structure and squareness to a project.

- **Painted projects:** Use hardwood underlayment.
- **Stained projects:** Match the species (e.g., oak plywood with oak furniture).

### 3/4" Plywood

Used for structural projects: bookshelves, cabinets, dressers, closets. Strong; cabinet-grade has a smooth veneer face.

- Cuts must be precise.
- **Painted projects:** Fill and sand exposed edges.
- **Stained projects:** Apply veneer-matched edge banding to exposed edges.

## Outdoor / Exterior Lumber

### Red-toned 2x (Recommended)

Dense, absorbs moisture slowly, holds fasteners well, produces strong and stable joints. Lasts 10-20 years.

### Yellow-toned 2x (Avoid)

Softer, lighter, expands and contracts more, pushes fasteners out over time leading to wobbly joints.

### Cedar (Use with Caution)

Naturally rot-resistant but soft. Joints may loosen over time.

### Decking

Use cedar or 1x boards. Install bark side up. Seal against pooling water.
